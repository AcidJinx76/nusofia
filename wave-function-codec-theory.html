The Wave Function Is Not What You Think It Is

On superposition, collapse, and the ontological status of ψ — read through the Codec Theory

Michele Zampighi · Phi Kosmos Developments LLC · Abu Dhabi

⁂

For nearly a century, physics has asked the same question about the wave function and received no satisfactory answer: what is ψ?

Is it a real object in the world — a field, a substance, something that exists independently of observation? Or is it a description of what we know — a tool for calculating probabilities, with no ontological weight of its own?

The realists say ψ is ontic. The Bayesians say it is epistemic. The many-worlders say it never collapses. Copenhagen says it does. QBism says the question is about the agent, not the world. Each interpretation resolves one problem by creating another. The measurement problem migrates; it is never dissolved.

Nusofia offers a third position that does not coincide with any of the above — and that, if taken seriously, reframes the entire debate.

⁂

I. The codec and the compression

The Codec Theory, formalised as a category-theoretic extension of the Nusofian ontology, distinguishes two levels of description:

P — fundamental reality: a dimensionless, atemporal, aspatial field of pure relationality. All compatible configurations coexist simultaneously. There is no space, no time, no separation. The metaphor from the corpus is the piano with all keys pressed at once: every note sounds, but no melody is distinguishable.

E — the phenomenal world: what an observer experiences. E is P compressed through a functor F — the codec — which maps the infinite relational structure of P into a navigable, finite representation. Space, time, extension, locality, separability: these are parameters of the codec, not features of the real.

Every observer has a computational constraint B (Bremermann-type) that determines the resolution and character of the compression. Different codecs lose different information. The set of information lost — the kernel of the functor — is what makes each phenomenal world partial and each observer structurally alone.

This architecture has a direct and radical consequence for the wave function.

⁂

II. ψ as compression

The Nusofian claim is this:

ψ is the least lossy compression that our codec can produce of the relational structure of P.

It is not an object in the world. But neither is it merely subjective. It captures real structure — relational, pre-spatial, pre-temporal structure — translated into the only language capable of approximating it: mathematics.

Mathematics, in the Nusofian framework, is the codec with the smallest kernel — the one that loses the least information in the compression from P to representation. This is not a metaphor. It is a structural claim: the reason ψ is written in mathematics is not convention or convenience. It is necessity. Only the least lossy codec can approach the pre-phenomenal structure closely enough to describe it. Any other language — visual, verbal, sensory — has already compressed too far.

But — and this is a correction that emerged in dialogue during the development of the Codec Theory — mathematics is a bit more lossless, not perfect. It still loses. ψ is the best available approximation of P, not P itself. The incompleteness of quantum mechanics is not a technical problem awaiting a future solution. It is a structural property of any codec, mathematical or otherwise. Gödel from the formal side, Heisenberg from the physical side, and the Codec Theory from the ontological side all converge on the same limit: no compression can be total.

⁂

III. Why ψ is complex

One of the features of the wave function that resists intuition is its mathematical form. ψ takes values in the complex numbers. It has amplitude and phase. The phase has no direct sensory correlate — you cannot see it, hear it, feel it.

The standard response is pragmatic: complex numbers work, and that is sufficient. The Nusofian response is structural: ψ should be unrepresentable in sensory terms, because it describes relational structure that exists prior to the codec that generates sensory experience. The phase of ψ encodes relational information that has no translation into spatial or temporal terms — not because it is abstract, but because space and time do not exist at the level it describes.

The mathematical codec can name the phase. The perceptual codec cannot render it. This gap between naming and rendering is not a failure of intuition. It is the signature of a codec reaching beyond its own resolution — which is exactly what happens when the least lossy language describes a structure that all other languages have already compressed away.

⁂

IV. Superposition is not the mystery

The default question in quantum mechanics is: how can something be in two states at once?

Nusofia inverts the question.

In P, all mutually compatible configurations coexist. This is not a hypothesis about quantum mechanics — it is the foundational ontological claim of the framework, articulated in the Incipit of the corpus: reality is a dimensionless Point that simultaneously assumes all possible states, and coherent configurations emerge only through an observer who shares their compatibility principle.

Superposition is not the anomaly. It is the natural state. What requires explanation is not the coexistence of states but their resolution — the fact that in E, an observer registers a single outcome.

The codec provides the explanation. An observer with constraint B compresses P into E. The compression selects a configuration that is internally coherent with the observer's own structure — its own Principle of Reality. What physics calls "measurement" is not a physical event that happens to a particle. It is the act of compression itself: the functor F mapping a region of P into a definite element of E.

No mechanism is required. No additional postulate. The Born rule, the projection postulate, the collapse — they are descriptions of what the compression looks like from inside E. From outside — from the structural perspective of the Codec Theory — there is no collapse. There is only the passage from one level of description to another.

V. The measurement problem dissolves

The measurement problem, stated precisely, is this: the Schrödinger equation is linear and deterministic. It describes unitary evolution. But measurement outcomes are discrete and probabilistic. How does one produce the other?

Every interpretation of quantum mechanics is, at bottom, a different answer to this question. Copenhagen says: collapse is real, accept it as an axiom. Many Worlds says: collapse is illusory, all branches exist. QBism says: collapse is an update of the agent's beliefs, not a physical process. Decoherence says: the environment effectively selects outcomes, though the mechanism of selection remains debated.

The Codec Theory dissolves the problem by identifying a category error in the question itself.

The Schrödinger equation describes evolution in P — or rather, it describes what the mathematical codec can capture of the relational dynamics in P. This evolution is continuous, linear, and unitary because the relational structure of P has no intrinsic discontinuities. There is nothing there to "jump."

The measurement outcome is a datum in E — a specific configuration registered by a specific codec. The transition from continuous evolution to discrete outcome is not a physical event. It is the transition from P-level description to E-level description. It is the functor operating.

Asking "why does ψ collapse?" is like asking why a high-resolution source file becomes a compressed image when you open it on a device with limited bandwidth. Nothing happens to the source file. The device renders what it can. The rest is in the kernel.

⁂

VI. The Born rule and the kernel

Why does |ψ|² give the correct probabilities? The Born rule is one of the most empirically successful and least understood axioms in physics. It is typically accepted as a brute fact — a postulate that works but that no interpretation has derived from deeper principles without circularity.

The Codec Theory suggests a direction. If ψ is the compression of P, and the kernel of the functor is the information lost in that compression, then the distribution of accessible configurations in E is determined by the structure of the kernel. What the observer can see depends on what the codec throws away.

|ψ|² may be the mathematical signature of this structure — the trace of the kernel projected into the space of possible outcomes. The squared modulus would then not be an arbitrary choice or a convenient axiom but a necessary consequence of the geometry of compression.

This is speculative. It is not a theorem. But it is a speculation with a definite direction, which is more than any interpretation currently offers for the Born rule. A derivation would require formalising the kernel of the functor F in category-theoretic terms and showing that the natural measure on the image of F reproduces |ψ|². This is open work — and it constitutes one of the most concrete falsifiability paths for the Codec Theory.

VII. The three interpretations as partial projections

The deepest consequence of the Nusofian reading is that it does not choose among the existing interpretations. It contains them — as partial projections of the same structure, each capturing one face and missing the others.

Copenhagen says collapse is real. It is right that something happens at the moment of measurement — but wrong to locate it in the physical world. What happens is not a physical event but the compression of the codec. Copenhagen describes the transition from P to E without recognising that two levels are involved.

Many Worlds says all branches coexist and no collapse occurs. It is right that in P all compatible configurations coexist — this is the foundational ontological claim of Nusofia. But it is wrong to project this coexistence into E as spatiotemporally parallel "worlds." The other branches are not worlds. They are the configurations that remain in the kernel of the functor — real in P, inaccessible in E. Not because they are far away, but because the codec cannot render them.

QBism says ψ is about the agent, not the world. It is right that the codec is observer-dependent — different observers with different constraints B produce different compressions. But it is wrong to reduce this to subjective belief. The codec compresses real relational structure. It is not an opinion about P. It is a structural, constrained, information-losing map of P. What the agent "believes" is determined by the architecture of the functor, not by epistemic freedom.

Each interpretation is a shadow of the Codec Theory projected onto a wall that has room for only one dimension. Nusofia provides the geometry in which all three shadows originate.

VIII. Wigner, mathematics, and the least lossy codec

In 1960, Eugene Wigner published his famous essay on the "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences." The question was: why does mathematics describe the physical world so well? It seemed like a coincidence — or, worse, a mystery that no philosophy of science could explain.

The Codec Theory provides a structural answer.

Mathematics is not unreasonably effective. It is the codec with the smallest kernel — the representational system that loses the least in the compression from P to description. ψ is written in mathematics not because physicists chose to write it that way, but because only the least lossy codec can approach the pre-phenomenal structure closely enough to describe what other codecs have already compressed away.

The effectiveness is not unreasonable. It is necessary. And its limits are equally necessary: the mathematical codec, being a codec, still has a kernel. It still loses. Gödel's incompleteness, Heisenberg's uncertainty, and the interpretive underdetermination of quantum mechanics are all signatures of the same structural fact — the fact that no map, however refined, is the territory.

Wigner was right to be astonished. But the astonishment dissolves once you recognise that what makes mathematics special is not a mysterious harmony between mind and world, but a measurable property of a compression functor: minimal information loss.

IX. What this means for physics

The Nusofian reading of ψ makes no claim to replace quantum mechanics as a computational tool. The Schrödinger equation, the Born rule, the commutation relations — they continue to do what they have always done with extraordinary precision.

What the reading does is change what we think these tools are about.

ψ is not a description of the world. It is not a description of our knowledge. It is a structural compression of a relational field that has no space, no time, and no separability — performed by the one codec that can approach it without collapsing into sensory metaphor. The measurement problem is not a problem. The collapse is not an event. Superposition is not a mystery. Non-locality is not spooky — it is the inevitable consequence of a reality in which spatial separation does not exist at the fundamental level.

If this reading is correct, then the century-long interpretive war in quantum mechanics has been a war over shadows — over which projection of P onto E is "real." The answer is: none of them and all of them. Each interpretation correctly describes one aspect of how the codec operates. None of them describes P itself, because P cannot be described in E-level language. It can only be compressed — more or less faithfully — by the codec that asks the least of the signal and discards the least of the answer.

A note on falsifiability

The reading proposed here generates at least two concrete paths toward empirical engagement:

1. The kernel derivation of the Born rule. If the Codec Theory is correct, |ψ|² should be derivable from the structure of the functor's kernel, not postulated as an axiom. A successful derivation would be strong evidence; a proof that no such derivation is possible would be strong counter-evidence.

2. Observer-dependent physics. If the codec is observer-dependent and the computational constraint B determines the parameters of the compression (including the physical constants experienced), then observers with radically different B should, in principle, encounter different effective physics. This is not currently testable with human observers — but the existence of artificial observers with different B (AI systems) opens a theoretical line of inquiry: do different computational architectures, processing the same quantum data, produce systematically different phenomenal structures?

Neither path is trivial. Both are definite. This is not a framework that hides from refutation. It is one that specifies, with unusual precision, the conditions under which it would fail.
