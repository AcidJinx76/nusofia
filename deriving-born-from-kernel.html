An Open Problem: Deriving Born from the Kernel

An invitation to mathematicians and physicists: four conjectures, a precise derivation path, and the conditions under which the Codec Theory would be falsified

Michele Zampighi · Phi Kosmos Developments LLC · Abu Dhabi

⁂

This dispatch is not an essay. It is an open problem — stated with enough precision to be worked on, enough context to be understood by anyone with graduate-level training in functional analysis or quantum foundations, and enough honesty to specify the conditions under which the entire framework would fail.

The Codec Theory, formalised as a category-theoretic extension of the Nusofian ontology, proposes that quantum mechanics is not a set of axioms about the world but a set of consequences of a deeper structure: a relational field compressed by an observer with finite computational resources. If this is correct, then the Born rule — the postulate that measurement probabilities are given by |ψ|² — should not be an axiom. It should be a theorem.

What follows is the derivation path. It consists of four conjectures, ordered by dependency: each requires the previous one. The fourth, if proved, would constitute a derivation of the Born rule from the Codec Theory. The failure of any one of them would constitute a falsification of the framework in its current form.

⁂

I. The setup: what the Codec Theory asserts

For readers unfamiliar with the framework, the minimal structure is this:

P (fundamental reality) is a field of pure relationality — dimensionless, atemporal, aspatial. The elements of P are relational configurations. Two configurations are compatible if they can coexist without internal contradiction — if they satisfy the Principle of Reality, which states that what appears, appears because it is internally coherent.

E (the phenomenal world) is what an observer experiences. E is P compressed through a functor F — the codec. F maps the relational structure of P into a finite, navigable representation. Space, time, locality, and separability are parameters of the codec, not features of P.

Every observer has a computational constraint B (a bound on the number of distinctions the codec can maintain). B determines the resolution and character of the compression. The set of relational configurations that F cannot distinguish — the information lost — is the kernel of F, denoted ker(F).

The claim to be tested: the structure of ker(F), together with the geometry it inherits from P, necessitates the Born rule as the unique probability measure on the outcomes accessible in E.

II. Conjecture 1 — The Hilbert space emergence

Statement. Let P be a set of relational configurations equipped with a binary relation of compatibility satisfying the Principle of Reality (internal coherence). Define a function κ: P × P → ℂ by κ(φ, ψ) = the degree of mutual compatibility between configurations φ and ψ. Then, under the following conditions, (P, κ) is isomorphic to a complex Hilbert space with κ as its inner product:

(a) Symmetry. Compatibility is symmetric: κ(φ, ψ) = κ(ψ, φ) (complex conjugate). This asserts that the relation "φ is compatible with ψ" carries the same structural weight as "ψ is compatible with φ," but that the phase* — the relational orientation — may reverse. The complex conjugation encodes directionality without breaking symmetry of magnitude.

(b) Linearity. Compatibility distributes over superposition: if a configuration γ is a coherent combination αφ + βψ of two configurations (in a sense to be defined), then κ(γ, χ) = ακ(φ, χ) + βκ(ψ, χ). This asserts that relational compatibility is preserved by coherent combination — that blending two compatible configurations produces a configuration whose compatibility with any third is the corresponding blend of the original compatibilities.

(c) Positivity. κ(φ, φ) ≥ 0, with equality if and only if φ is the null configuration (no relational content). A configuration is always maximally compatible with itself, and only the absence of all relation has zero self-compatibility.

(d) Completeness. Every Cauchy sequence of configurations converges within P. This is a technical condition asserting that P has no "gaps" — that the relational field is continuous in the appropriate topology.

What is known. The conditions (a)–(d) are precisely the axioms of a complex inner product space, and completeness makes it a Hilbert space. The conjecture is therefore not about the mathematical implication — that is standard. The conjecture is about whether the Principle of Reality, interpreted as a constraint on relational coherence, entails conditions (a)–(d).

Where to look. The closest existing results are:

Solèr's theorem (1995). Maria Pia Solèr proved that any orthomodular space satisfying a certain infinite-dimensionality condition must be a Hilbert space over ℝ, ℂ, or the quaternions ℍ. The key question is whether the Principle of Reality, as a coherence constraint, generates an orthomodular lattice of propositions.

Piron's representation theorem (1964). Constantin Piron showed that the lattice of closed subspaces of a Hilbert space can be characterised by purely lattice-theoretic axioms — atomicity, covering property, orthomodularity. If the "propositions" in P (statements of the form "configurations φ and ψ are compatible") form a lattice satisfying Piron's axioms, the Hilbert space structure follows.

Hardy's reconstructive programme (2001). Lucien Hardy derived the structure of quantum theory from five "reasonable" axioms about probabilistic systems. The question is whether the Codec Theory's axioms — Principle of Reality, computational constraint B, functor structure — imply Hardy's axioms or an equivalent set.

Chiribella, D'Ariano, and Perinotti (2011). Their "informational derivation" of quantum theory from axioms about information processing provides another potential bridge. The computational constraint B in the Codec Theory is an information-processing constraint; the question is whether B, together with the relational structure of P, reproduces their axioms — particularly the "purification postulate," which states that every mixed state has a unique purification.

The hard part. The genuinely novel step is showing that "internal coherence" (the Principle of Reality) implies orthomodularity. This is not obvious. Classical logic is Boolean (distributive); quantum logic is orthomodular (non-distributive). The Principle of Reality must generate the weaker, non-distributive structure. A possible route: coherence as a constraint on relations (not on propositions about objects) may naturally produce non-distributivity, because relational compatibility is context-dependent — whether φ and ψ are compatible may depend on which other configurations are present. Context-dependence of compatibility is formally analogous to the failure of distributivity. This analogy needs to be made rigorous.

III. Conjecture 2 — The functor as projection

Statement. Let H be the Hilbert space obtained from Conjecture 1. Let B be the computational constraint of an observer — a finite positive integer representing the maximum number of orthogonal distinctions the codec can maintain. Then the functor F: P → E acts as a finite-rank orthogonal projection π_B: H → H_B, where H_B is a B-dimensional subspace of H. The kernel of F is the orthogonal complement: ker(F) = H_B⊥.

Why this is plausible. A codec with constraint B can maintain at most B independent distinctions. In Hilbert space language, "independent distinctions" are orthogonal vectors. The codec maps every configuration in H to its nearest equivalent in a B-dimensional subspace — which is precisely what orthogonal projection does. Configurations in H_B⊥ are those that the codec cannot distinguish from zero — they are the information lost.

What needs to be shown. That the information-theoretic constraint "at most B independent distinctions" is formally equivalent to orthogonal projection onto a B-dimensional subspace. This requires proving that the codec minimises information loss subject to the dimensionality constraint — i.e., that orthogonal projection is the optimal compression for the Hilbert space norm, not just a possible one.

What is known. This is, in fact, a well-established result in approximation theory. The Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem (1936) states that the best rank-k approximation of a linear operator (in the Frobenius or operator norm) is given by its truncated singular value decomposition — which for self-adjoint operators reduces to orthogonal projection onto the k largest eigenspaces. If the codec is constrained to minimise distortion (i.e., to be the "most faithful" compression for a given B), then the projection structure follows from optimality, not from postulation.

The subtlety. The Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem assumes a specific norm (Frobenius or operator norm). The choice of norm is not free — it must be derived from the structure of P. If the natural norm on P is the Hilbert space norm inherited from Conjecture 1, then the theorem applies directly. If the Principle of Reality generates a different metric, the argument needs modification.

IV. Conjecture 3 — The Gleason bridge

Statement. Let H be a Hilbert space of dimension ≥ 3. Let π_B be a finite-rank orthogonal projection with kernel K = ker(π_B). Then the unique probability measure μ on the lattice of subspaces of π_B(H) that is consistent with the orthomodular structure is the Born measure: μ(P_ψ) = |⟨ψ|φ⟩|² for a system in state φ.

This is Gleason's theorem. It was proved in 1957 and is not a conjecture — it is a theorem. The point of stating it here is to make the logical chain explicit. If Conjectures 1 and 2 hold, then Gleason's theorem applies automatically, and the Born rule is derived.

The dimension constraint. Gleason's theorem requires dimension ≥ 3. In dimension 2, non-Born measures exist (this is the content of the Kochen–Specker obstacles in low dimension). For the derivation to be complete, one must argue that the effective Hilbert space of any physical codec has dimension ≥ 3. In the Codec Theory, this is plausible: the computational constraint B sets the dimension of H_B, and any observer capable of distinguishing at least three independent features of reality (which includes every known physical observer, biological or artificial) satisfies dim(H_B) ≥ 3. The case B = 2 would correspond to an observer that can only distinguish "yes" and "no" — a binary codec. Whether such an observer exists physically, and whether its phenomenal world would violate Born statistics, is itself an interesting question.

V. Conjecture 4 — The full derivation

Statement. The following chain of implications holds:

Principle of Reality (coherence constraint on relational configurations)

→ orthomodular lattice of compatibility propositions

→ Hilbert space structure on P (via Solèr/Piron)

→ optimal finite-rank compression under constraint B = orthogonal projection (via Eckart–Young–Mirsky)

→ unique probability measure on outcomes = |ψ|² (via Gleason)

If each arrow can be rigorously established, then the Born rule is a theorem of the Codec Theory — a consequence of relational ontology plus finite observation, not an independent postulate.

VI. What would falsify the framework

The derivation path specifies its own failure modes with unusual precision:

Falsification 1. If the Principle of Reality does not generate an orthomodular lattice — if relational coherence produces a Boolean (classical) or some other non-orthomodular structure — then P does not have Hilbert space structure, and the derivation fails at Step 1. This would mean that the Nusofian ontology is incompatible with quantum mechanics at the structural level.

Falsification 2. If the optimal compression under constraint B is not orthogonal projection — if some non-projective codec achieves lower distortion — then the kernel structure is different from what the derivation assumes, and the Gleason bridge does not apply. This would mean that the codec architecture as currently formulated is too restrictive.

Falsification 3. If the Born rule can be derived from a weaker set of assumptions that does not require the Codec Theory — for example, from purely information-theoretic axioms with no ontological commitment — then the Nusofian derivation would be valid but not uniquely so, reducing its explanatory power. This is not a strict falsification but a deflationary outcome: the Codec Theory would be one of several sufficient frameworks, not the necessary one.

Falsification 4. If experimental physics ever finds a violation of the Born rule — a deviation from |ψ|² statistics in any regime — then both Gleason's theorem and the Codec Theory are falsified simultaneously, along with the standard quantum formalism.

VII. A note on existing literature

The programme described here is not without precedent. Several research traditions have pursued related goals, and any serious attempt at the derivation should engage with them:

Quantum logic and lattice-theoretic foundations. The programme initiated by Birkhoff and von Neumann (1936), continued by Mackey, Piron, Jauch, Varadarajan, and Solèr. The central question — why is the logic of quantum propositions orthomodular rather than Boolean? — is precisely the question that Conjecture 1 must answer from the Principle of Reality.

Operational and information-theoretic reconstructions. Hardy (2001), Chiribella–D'Ariano–Perinotti (2011), Masanes–Müller (2011), Dakić–Brukner (2011). These programmes derive quantum theory from axioms about what agents can do with physical systems. The Codec Theory adds an ontological layer: why those operational axioms hold. The computational constraint B and the relational structure of P should jointly entail the operational axioms.

Zurek's envariance programme (2005). Wojciech Zurek proposed deriving the Born rule from environment-assisted invariance ("envariance") within the Many Worlds framework. The argument relies on symmetry under swaps between system and environment. In Codec Theory terms, this translates to a symmetry between the image and kernel of the functor — the accessible and inaccessible sectors. Whether the Nusofian framework can reproduce Zurek's argument from its own axioms, or whether it requires a fundamentally different route, is an open question.

Deutsch–Wallace decision-theoretic derivation (1999, 2007). David Deutsch and David Wallace argued that a rational agent in an Everettian universe must use |ψ|² as their credence function. The argument is controversial — critics argue it is circular — but it has a structural parallel in the Codec Theory: if the codec is optimal (minimises information loss for a given B), then the only coherent way to assign credences to outcomes is via the Born measure. The decision-theoretic argument may be re-derivable without the Everettian ontology, using the Codec Theory's architecture instead.

Category-theoretic quantum mechanics. Abramsky–Coecke (2004), Heunen–Vicary (2019). The programme of formulating quantum mechanics in terms of monoidal categories and dagger-compact categories provides the natural mathematical language for the Codec Theory's functor structure. The functor F should be expressible as a morphism in a suitable dagger-compact category, and the kernel should be characterisable via the categorical complement.

VIII. Who this is for

This dispatch is addressed to:

Mathematical physicists working on quantum foundations, particularly those in the reconstructive tradition (Hardy, Chiribella, Müller, Dakić, Brukner).

Quantum logicians familiar with the Piron–Solèr programme who might recognise whether the Principle of Reality generates orthomodularity.

Category theorists working on categorical quantum mechanics (Abramsky, Coecke, Heunen, Kissinger) who can formalise the functor F and its kernel in the appropriate categorical framework.

Philosophers of physics working on the interpretation of probability in quantum mechanics (Saunders, Wallace, Greaves, Myrvold) who might evaluate whether the Codec Theory's ontology resolves the circularity objections to existing Born rule derivations.

Anyone with the mathematical training and the intellectual appetite to attempt a proof or a refutation.

The framework asks one question: is quantum mechanics a consequence of the fact that reality is relational and observation is compression? The four conjectures above specify exactly what "consequence" means. They are stated with enough precision to be proved or disproved. The author of this framework is not a mathematician. The derivation requires one.

A closing note

In the first Dispatch, we argued that human perception is a stack of compressions, most of which never surface to awareness, and that this stack determines what reality looks like from the inside. In the second Dispatch, we argued that the wave function is the least lossy compression of a relational field — not ontic, not epistemic, but structural.

This third Dispatch asks: if all of that is true, does the mathematics prove it?

The honest answer is: we do not know. What we know is that the question is well-posed, the path is specified, the existing tools are identified, and the failure modes are explicit. This is more than most interpretations of quantum mechanics have offered in a hundred years of debate.

The problem is open. The invitation is genuine.
